Overview

During my time on the Coding Bootcamp, I expressed an interest for designing any of the websites that we would build as part of our development projects. When the Bootcamp was over, my developer friend Josh asked me to help him design an application and website he was working on for a drumming charity called the Palace Drum Clinic. Willing to help my friend and help a charity, I jumped at this opportunity.


I drove straight into designing the mockup. I thoroughly enjoy designing website and app mockups - but at this time, this was just graphic design - I had no knowledge of what UX was and the process. I made my first mock up of the application, and it was bad. I then started studying the UX design process, but it was just minimal and free resources that I found on YouTube - I had not invested in my learning at this point. I then had a very vague idea on the process (competitive analysis, user personas etc), and decided to incorporate the minimal knowledge I knew into this drumming project. My designs improved tremendously.

Present day me has had the wonderful opportunity to speak with many UX Designers, Researchers and Architects since making this project. I have also done lots of studying with online teachers such as CareerFoundary, KickassUX and Coursera. I now understand the value of doing the UX processes properly from start to finish, and how it actually helps to save so much time. So the project you are about to view really encompasses my development. It represents me in my infancy of UX, and more focused on UI designs. I will talk about things I would do differently now that I am more passionate and knowledgable on UX throughout...

The Challenge

Design a user-friendly and engaging application that empowers users to improve their drumming skills through high-quality video tutorials and support from high-class drumming teachers. The application must be scalable to a mobile and a desktop version. As this is for a charity, it has a very limited budget, and is centered around helping the community.

The First Designs

As mentioned in the overview, the first thing I did was dive straight into designing the mockup using Photoshop. This was before I knew anything about the UX Design process. I had no idea who my users were, what features I was designing, the architecture of the app - I had no guidelines.

I just started designing what I liked, what I thought would work, with no thought given to any users. I was just thinking of the developer and what would be simple for him to recreate. I was completely biased in the mind of a developer. No wonder it looked pants...

Starting Again

After doing some studying of the UX step process, I decided to start from scratch. I wanted to ensure a user-centered approach, gain insights and develop a better, more organised product.

User Research

I wanted to better understand who the users I was designing for would be.

1. Understand users knowledge and opinions of current drumming applications.
2. What features would they find useful.
3. What friction points do drumming learners currently have.

I surveyed those that currently work for the drum clinic and a selection of those that are beginners to drumming or are intermediate learners (54 users). I gave these users 10 questions to gain quantitative insights. Here were the results:

95% Use YouTube as their prime educational resource for drumming related topics
40% Take drumming lessons with a tutor virtually
66% Would like to attend more drumming related events
Only 20% could name a competitor that was not YouTube

blah blah blah

User Personas

A primary user persona was created using the user research information. This was really useful in making sure I designed for my users, and not for myself.

Competitive Analysis

Two competitors were frequently mentioned in the initial discussion and user research; these competitors were YouTube and Drumeo, which offer both a desktop and mobile version of their product.

YouTube - a famous platform that offers free, educational videos made by creators

Drumeo - Offers a specialised drum curriculum with lessons of all skill levels

We wanted our drum application to combine as many strengths of our competitors as possible, whilst aiming to be a unique product on the market.

Framing the problem

In order to overview and frame the problem, I set out by completing a time-boxed (10 minute) whiteboard session. I wanted to define the user needs and goals, frustrations, assumptions and constraints.

User Needs
-Someone who feels lonely / social anxiety
-Wants new friends
-Feels isolated, shy, difficulty initiating conversations

Frustrations & Pain Points
-Difficulty finding friends with things in common
-Limited opportunities to meet new people
-Fear of rejection & social anxiety
-Lack of safe and supportive platforms to connect with like-minded people

User Goals
-Solution to loneliness and social anxiety:
the main goal of the app is to provide a solution for people who feel lonely/social anxiety by helping them make meaningful connections
-The app keeps users safe
-Environment is inclusive
-Users can overcome social barriers and find companionship

Assumptions
-Users are seeking to make new friends and are open to engaging in conversation and activities with potential friends
-Users are comfortable with using a mobile app
-Matching users on shared social interests increases likelihood of forming friendships
-Provide a platform that offers support and resources for social anxiety to assist users

Constraints
-Mobile app/latest iOS
-Keep data secure
-Budget and timeframe
-3 weeks to build
-Small engineering team

When I first learnt about whiteboard sessions, I will be honest, I thought it was just a silly idea. How could anything of value come out of me scribbling for 10 minutes? I was wrong. I found pushing myself in a confined time frame to really get out the initial thoughts and feelings that I didn't realise I had! The time pressure really forced my mind to think. In a real life scenario, I could see how this would be useful to do a whiteboard session with a project manager or project owner who might be crunched for time. This really helped me to progress to future stages.

How Might We?

The developer and I discussed some HMW prompts, to address specific challenges and opportunities around the app and develop user-centered solutions.

HMW facilitate meaningful connections between users and drumming teachers?
HMW encourage users to participate in drumming events and activities to create a community and collaboration?
HMW make the drumming tutorials engaging and interactive for users of all skill levels?

The process you are about to view is for an app I have named Aloha. Aloha means "hello" in Hawaiian and it is also a way of living: treating others with kindess, respect and peace. I was the sole designer from start to finish and the whole process took around two months. The tools I utilised were: Survey Monkey, Balsamiq, Figma & Miro.

Research & Discovery

Pit-Stop Reflections

I had not been following the UX process properly through my development so far. I was using parts of the process that I had briefly researched about before, such as user personas and competitive analysis - which I had found was really helpful. I wished I had followed the UX design process completely from start to finish. I found myself going back and forth - I just wanted to start designing.

Define & Ideate

Site map

The developer and I outlisted the the stages from my user journey and began creating the site map. We took our competitors into consideration, and tried to create the web-architecture based around the features that our app contains.

Low Fidelity Wireframing / Sketching

With my framing the problem whiteboard session turning out positive for me, I set myself a timer and did a quick whiteboard session to map out the basic wireframes of the application (crazy 8s style). This was my chance to express the immediate ideas I had in my mind out on paper. Don't think too much, just sketch!

I then took the above wireframes and developed them without the time pressure. Developing the wireframes in this way helped me to consider all the feedback from the initial user research and inspire new ideas as I went along, I made notes on my wireframes as new ideas came to mind, such as an option to 'wave' at matched friends - a low pressure way of starting a conversation.

I sat back and reflected on the wireframes above, and developed them once again without time pressure. I felt my designs and sketches had improved with every iteration - and more ideas came to me each time. The app was growing like a rolling snowball - I had limited time, and was only one designer, it was time to reign it in.

Mid Fidelity Wireframing

I then looked over my low-fidelity wireframes and felt overwhelmed by the amount of information on them. It is great to have some notes to refer to for future development, but it was time to make my designs more succinct. I needed my wireframes to be straight forward so that developers, clients, designers... and myself... get a clear understanding of the functionalities, and overall design. I translated the sketches to low-fidelity wireframes using Balsamiq for better detail and screen accuracy.

App Onboarding and Profile Setup

View friend matches and chatting to new friends

View and create events, forums & community groups and view blogs

Edit profile details, view saved groups and check notifications

I really enjoyed using Balsamiq to create these wireframes. All of the icons and feature templates (such as photo, loading bar, nav bar etc) are pre-made. It was a simple case of picking the appropriate feature and matching it to my sketches. In the past, I would have made all the features from scratch myself, so using Balsamiq was much more time-effective.

Pit-Stop Reflections

With my wireframes in hand, it would be ideal at this stage to complete some user testing on them. As this was a solo UX development project, I did not have others on hand to help me with this. However, I understand how vital it is to get critism and feedback, and make important alterations, before the high-fidelity product is designed. Instead, I considered my user personas and what they might think.

I was suprised at the speedy progress I was making. Before, when I have jumped straight into the design process, it would have taken me twice as long to get to a final product. I thought that all of this researching, and wireframing process, would make things go even slower. But actually, I had all of my notes and my sketches ready and I felt confident to begin the high-fidelity prototyping stage. On previous design projects, I would have jumped straight into the high-fidelity prototyping stage (because this is the fun part) but then find myself hitting a road block and asking myself things like: Where is this page meant to lead next? What features am I designing again?

Style Guide

As I was creating my app, I built the style guide as I went along. I had my colours, spacing and fonts prepared ahead of designing the high-fidelity frames, as I had checked these for accessibility. The chosen colour scheme also had a hidden meaning: the primary teal colour represents a sense of calmness, trust and friendliness. The inspiring cyan shade is associated with liveliness, youth and energy. These colours also remind me of the ocean, which coincides with the spirit of Aloha.

As I created my cards and buttons, I added them to my style guide, which acted as a design system - this prevented me from re-making items I had already made (such as cards) and they were available when I needed them, therefore I had reusable components which made the design process even speedier.

My design system helped me to save time, keep things consistent, improve the overall look of the app and assist a developer who might build the app. As a developer myself, I am biased to the previous point in particular - having repeatable components makes developing an application much less painful and time consuming.

High Fidelity Wireframes

Using Figma, the mid-fidelity wireframes were transformed into high-fidelity wireframes, and then an interactive prototype. After adding improvements, colour, finer details, and making a very large mistake (which I will tell you about in my learning and reflections section), my wireframes were ready.

The user will personalise their profile by adding photos of themselves and writing a bio. The user can opt to pick experiences (such as religion, education, politics), and pick interests (such as reading, reggae and rock climbing) - this feeds the algorithm and matches uses based on similar interests.

The matching algorithm is also controlled by prompts: a user picks up to five of these prompts, which are generated based on the interests they chose. For example, if you selected hiking as an interest, the user can pick a prompt called 'A recent hiking adventure' where they can add a photo of a view and the location. This makes the profilemore interesting to read and is more personable to other users.

When a user is ready to start viewing friend matches, they are not initially shown any photos of the potential friend - this is to avoid judgement and 'window-shopping', instead the user is shown the potential friend's name and their matching interests. The user can choose to view the potential friend's profile, where they will see photos, bio, prompts etc - or they can wave to the potential friend, which is a low-pressure way of initiating conversation.

Age is also a feature that is not shown on anyones profile - instead a user can opt to show their star sign. The app is all about compatibility and friendship - and age could be a discriminating factor. I would have liked to have tested this with potential users and received their feedback on this.

When a user views a potential friend's profile, their personalised prompts will show ice-breakers, which encourages the user to begin a conversation. For example, if a potential friend has chosen baking as an interest and their corresponding prompt is 'My favourite thing to bake' with a photo of some brownies, the ice-breaker prompt will show the user 'Ask [potential friend] for the recipe!' - when the ice-breaker is clicked, the user will be taken to the messaging area of the app.

The idea of ice-breakers is to help avoid awkward, boring conversations and make meaningful connections - it also encourages the user to show an interest in the potential friend, rather than just talk about themselves.

For those that want to make friendships in an organic way outside of the app, users can find events that are based on their interests and location. Users can also discover local community groups which offer smaller collections of people that meet frequently.

For virtual friendships with like-minded people that may be located further away, forums offer a great low-pressure way to connect with those of similar interests.

Curated blogs offer information and support on making friends, especially for those who have social anxiety. Blogs also offer advice for those that want to set up their own events, forums and community groups, as well as success stories on friendships made via Aloha.

Next Steps

Future Development

I would like to do user testing on my prototype and interate the design process to improve the app based on user feedback. I would also like to do some exploration around the algorithm portion of the app - would matching people based on interests really be successful? Would initially hiding the photos and hiding age really help prevent judgement and encourage genuine friendships? Bumble is the only friendship app with a complex algorithm that I can find information about, but all of the research I have done on this talks about the dating element of the app, and that algorithm is based on appearance and how frequently people get 'liked' - this does not align with the ethics of Aloha.

Conclusions

Learning & Reflections

I have thoroughly enjoyed this process from start to finish. It has been so informative on about UX design, the double diamond process and empathising with users.

As I have an engineering background, it has been nice to focus on the design side and let my imagination run free, without having the limitation of building the app. If I had to build this app, I would have set myself a ceiling with the designs, constantly thinking about how to make the app work behind the scenes, and this would have limited my designs and the complexity of the app.

I am happy with the overall app design and I feel my UI skills have improved. Maybe I am biased because this app has become my baby, but I would actually really like to try to build this app in React Native and make it come to life.

I learnt a lesson when I made a big mistake during the high-fidelity design stage. I prepared my frames and set them to iPhone 14 dimensions, and then designed each and every frame. When I was done, I began to make the interactive prototype - only to discover, my frames were not iPhone 14 size and did not fit inside the prototype template.

I am not sure how the dimensions changed, but I then had to go in and re-design every frame. I made sure to make the prototype at the same time to check everything was working and fit correctly. Unfortunately, it was not as easy as simply scaling the existing frames down, each element had to be resized and repositioned. I will never make that mistake again. That could have been extremely costly if this was a real life app that had a real deadline.

The positive of this mistake is that I had a chance to reflect on my designs again and make any improvements / adjustments. My skills with Figma improved and I felt my designs looked even better the second time around.

My top takeways:

Love of design: I have always loved designing apps and websites, and following the UX process made this go so much smoother and more enjoyable. I loved picking the colours and fonts, and creating the different components. I really put my heart into the designs.

The benefit of collaboration: In order to improve this app, I would love to get other designers and users involved. The app needs testing and feedback - my user personas can only take me so far before my own biases creep into the designs and operation of the app.

Check as you go: It is so important that I double check things such as the dimensions of my prototype, my spacing, my colours etc as I go. I do not want to design a whole app in the wrong dimensions ever again! This will allow me to watch out for mistakes and save valuable time.
