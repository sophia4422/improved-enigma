I read a fascinating book called 'Invisible Women' by Caroline Criado Perez, which discusses a gender data gap. We are making decisions and designing a world that is meant to work for everyone, but throughout history the decision makers and designers have been male. The book has a chapter on toilets...

When it comes to the design of a typical pubic bathroom, the needs and anatomy of women have seldom been considered, leading to lengthy loo queues.

I have investigated ways to improve the user experience for those using the women's restroom by considering women's wants and needs throughout the process.

Problem Discovery

What's taking so long?

When investigated, women take an average of 30 seconds longer in the bathroom than men on average. There are numerous reasons why women might take longer...


Women are more likely to be accompanied by children
20-25% of women of child baring age may be menstruating
Women make up the majority of the diabled and the elderly
Women are more likely to be accompanying or caring for the disabled / elderly
Women are more likely than men to wash their hands
Pregnancy increases the frequency of urination
Removal of clothing & redressing (rompers, stockings, trousers, underwear) takes longer

ANATOMY OF A BATHROOM

When it comes to the design of a public bathroom, more often or not you will find a separate male and female bathroom, where each room is given an equal amount of floor space.

Men more often have access to urinals, which are smaller than cubicles, so more urinals can fit in the same size space. Is equal floor space always so equal?

Pit-stop reflections

The lengthy queues women often wait makes perfect sense when you consider the facts, female anatomy, culture, combined with the notion that there are generally less toilets to use in the women's space.

What really shocked me during my research were the comments from the general public who often blame women for the long queues on things like doing makeup and talking too much, and shamed anyone who wanted to do something about it:

Proposed Solution

Gender Neutral Bathrooms

A study was led to solve the problem of a 6 minute wait time for women by two researchers: Kurt Vanhautegem and Wouter Rogiest. They proposed and tested a variety of layouts, with the optimum combining the spaces of male/female toilets to make one gender-neutral bathroom.

My first thought was while this reduced the women's average waiting time by almost 5 minutes, it increased the men's average waiting time by over 40 seconds, which some may argue is not completely fair.

It was also clear that the researchers had not considered women's thoughts and opinions in the design of this layout. So I decided to ask women what they thought of this solution...

User Testing / Feedback

I wanted to challenge my own biases and get the opinions and thoughts on this matter from women of different backgrounds. I started by sending out an anonymous survey with 6 questions to 10 women. Here are the results of the survey, including some of their feedback:

40% said they would feel uncomfortable entering a gender-neutral bathroom and seeing men using urinals
90% said they would use a gender-neutral bathroom that was made of stalls / cubicles only
All of the women said they use public bathrooms in various locations, with 20% saying they only use them if they are desperate

High Fidelity Wireframes

Heading straight into Figma, armed with only the house-style colours (which I had not even checked for accessibility), the site map and the user persona, I began designing my high-fidelity product and prototype. I cringe at the thought of this now.

The user can personalise their profile by adding a profile photo and cover photo. All personal details, such as email and subscription type, will only be visible to the user. When the user subscribes to the application, they will be able to access a variety of drumming tutorials and live shows, created by world class drummers.

Users can view drumming events, that have only been curated by the Palace Drum Clinic. A user can save an event they like the look of (this data will give marketers an idea of event interest), or they can buy tickets for the event - both saved and attending events will be shown only to the user on their profile.

Drumming tutorials are organised by their skill ability (beginner, intermediate etc) and their popularity amongst users. Videos can be viewed on demand and saved to a users personal playlist for later. Scheduled live shows will also be available to view. Videos do not only encompass tutorials, but also interviews and informal chats with the popular drummers, so users can get to know them on a more personal level.

Users can look through a list of the drumming artists available on the app and view their public profiles - this includes photos, a bio and the videos created or featuring said artist.

High Fidelity Prototype

Take a look at the prototype I have created in Figma. For a better experience, make the prototype full screen.

This prototype was presented to the PDC at their annual drum clinic where product owners feedback was received. The product was granted permission to be built, and currently it is in the works with the developer. Depending on how the community respond to the app will affect whether or not we can bring the product to life past the MVP and create more exciting and unique features.

Next Steps

Conclusions

Learning & Reflections

This was my first 'proper' design project, so to speak, and I would love to start all over again from the beginning and follow the UX design process properly to see what product I would create.



All of the research, planning and discussion is so valuable in order to design a viable and successful product. It is not all about how the product looks - I have learnt that lesson now.

When I get the chance to repeat this project again for the next stage of designs, I am going to do my utmost to involve as much testing as possible. This product is all about helping the community and inspire people to get involved in drumming, so it is important to get the community's feedback and advice at every stage of development.

My top takeways:

Don't rush into things: All I wanted to do was design the product. I have learnt that skipping steps, and not following the process properly (which in my defence, I didn't know how to do), leads to a poorly executed product. Planning, discussion and research are vital elements to get a better quality result.

Designing is like Science: I studied BioMed at Uni and I know the importance of hypothesing, planning and testing - I never thought that these sort of elements would be important in designing. Now I feel that these things are in many ways, more important than the final result itself, because without them, you do not get reliable, repeatable results.

Start from scratch: Hitting a road block? Feeling overwhelmed? Not getting anywhere? It is okay to just start again if you need to. I try so hard to strive for perfection, which is impossible, so I need to accept that. What you make the first time is not always going to be the best, and infact, doing things a few times makes you better at it. Making mistakes is vital to the learning process.
