The Challenge

Friends keep us company through the ups and downs of our lives - but as we grow older, our fear and social anxiety can keep us from making new friends. How might we empower individuals with social anxiety to overcome the first step out of their comfort zones in order to make new friends, receive companionship and alleviate feelings of loneliness?

Solution

Design and build an app based on friendship: allowing users to discover new people with similar interests and offer opportunities to attend events. The app will act as a friend itself, offering a safe and inclusive platform that enables like-minded users to connect. The app will allow users to curate a personalised profile about their hobbies and interests, and utilise personalised matching algorithms to meet others. Users can message one another in the app safely, and the app will offer optional ice-breaker prompts in order to initiate the conversation.

Additionally, the app will offer resources, communities and guidance to navigate users with social anxiety and boost their confidence. By creating an accessible and empowering digital space, the friendship-based app will facilitate personal growth, enhance well-being and alleviate loneliness.

Overview

This was a personal challenge that I set myself in order to learn more about the UX and research process, as well as improve my UI skills with Figma. I used an online prompt generator to come up with the task, and utilised online learning platforms such as CareerFoundry and LinkedIn Learning to learn the UX process from start to finish.

My role: I was the sole designer from user research through to the user interface
Timeline: 2 months
Tools: Survey Monkey, Procreate, Balsamiq, Figma, Miro

Research & Discovery

Competitive Analysis

I began the design journey by analysing the top three competitor apps in the friend-making-market.

Meetup: A popular platform that connects people through local events and activities

Bumble BFF: Find new friends based on common interests and values

Friender: Friend match-maker based on shared interests and hobbies

Overall, the friend-making app I am designing should aim to combine the strengths of its competitors.

I have learned that completing a competitive analysis is an excellent way to ensure that my app combines all of the useful features that have proven to already be successful, as well as avoid ones that are not. Taking the time to sit and study competitors has also proven an effective way to create inspiration for my own product, as well as ensure that I am not copying an exisiting product completely.

-Someone who feels lonely / social anxiety
-Wants new friends
-Feels isolated, shy, difficulty initiating conversations

Frustrations & Pain Points
-Difficulty finding friends with things in common
-Limited opportunities to meet new people
-Fear of rejection & social anxiety
-Lack of safe and supportive platforms to connect with like-minded people

User Goals
-Solution to loneliness and social anxiety:
the main goal of the app is to provide a solution for people who feel lonely/social anxiety by helping them make meaningful connections
-The app keeps users safe
-Environment is inclusive
-Users can overcome social barriers and find companionship

Assumptions
-Users are seeking to make new friends and are open to engaging in conversation and activities with potential friends

When I first learnt about whiteboard sessions, I thought it was just a silly idea. However, pushing myself in a confined time frame to really push out all of my initial thoughts amazed me - I didn't know I had that many thoughts already sitting in my head. The time pressure really forced my mind to think. I got so much benefit out such a small amount of time. In a real life scenario, I could see how this would be useful to whiteboard session with a project manager who might be crunched for time. This really helped me to progress to future stages.

User Research

With my competitive analysis complete and my problem framed, I surveyed a collection of potential users of the app and I asked them the following questions:

Do you find it difficult to make friends outside of a professional setting?

60% said they had difficulty making new friends outside of a professional setting

Do you face any challenges when talking to new people?

80% said they face challenges when talking to new people

Do you know many people who share the same interests as you?

60% said they don't know many people who share the same interests as them

Would you like to make friends with people that share common interests?

80% said they would like to make friends with people who share common interests

Would you be open to using an app to make friends?

60% would be open to using an app to make friends

When participants were asked what features they would like to see and what concerns they may have regarding the app, these were the most common answers:

Algorithm to match users based on personality
Messaging system for users to communicate
Forums / community groups based on particular interests
Ability to view events, plan events and be matched to events
Icebreaker activities / conversation starters
Inclusive, diverse and active user base

I turned the research answers into a word cloud to visualise the demand for certain features

User Personas

Three user personas were created to represent the typical users. It is important that we do not limit the app to those who are soley introverted as this may shy away those who are outgoing who are simply looking for new friends in a new area. We want this app to be inclusive, and encourage a diverse user-base, so it is important to encourage people of all walks of life to use it.

Whilst completing my UX research, I have found User Personas to be a very polarising concept amongst UX designers. Personally, I have found them to be incredibly useful - after making them, I found it to be a useful way of combining my user research and really eliminating any bias I had (concious and subconcious) to the design process. It kept empathy in the foreground, I am not designing for myself, or designing for what I like, I am designing for my users - and their needs vary from mine.

Define & Ideate

Sitemap

Next, I took the stages from my user journey map above, and created a sitemap to visualise the user journey. This acted as an efficient 'tick list' when the sketching process began, and when making my high-fidelity prototype later.

Low Fidelity Wireframing / Sketching

With my framing the problem whiteboard session turning out positive for me, I set myself a timer and did a quick whiteboard session to map out the basic wireframes of the application. This was my chance to express the immediate ideas I had in my mind out on paper. Don't think too much, just sketch!

I then took the above wireframes and developed them without the time pressure. Developing the wireframes in this way helped me to consider all the feedback from the research and come up with new ideas as I went along, I made notes on my wireframes as new ideas came to mind, such as an option to 'wave' at matched friends - a low pressure way of starting a conversation.

I sat back and reflected on the wireframes above, and developed them once again without time pressure. I felt my designs and sketches had improved with every iteration - and more ideas came to me each time. The app was growing like a rolling snowball - I had limited time, and was only one designer, it was time to reign it in.

Mid Fidelity Wireframing

I then reflected on these drawings and felt overwhelmed by the amount of information on them. It is great to have some notes to refer to for future development, but it was time to make my designs more succinct. I needed my wireframes to be clearer so that developers, clients, designers... and myself... get a clear understanding of the functionalities, and overall design look. So, I translated the sketches to low-fidelity wireframes using Balsamiq for better detail and screen accuracy:

App Onboarding and Profile Setup

View friend matches and chatting to new friends

View and create events, forums & community groups and view blogs

Edit profile details, view saved groups and check notifications

This was my first time using Balsamiq and I loved it! All of the icons and feature templates (such as photo, loading bar, nav bar etc) are pre-made. It was a simple case of picking the appropriate feature and matching it to my sketches. It was so time consuming vs in the past making all the features from scratch myself!

Mid-Way Reflections

With my wire-frames in hand, it would be ideal at this stage to complete some user testing on them. As this was a solo UX development project, I did not do this. However, I understand how vital it is to get critism and feedback, and make important alterations, before the high-fidelity product is designed. Instead, I considered my user personas and what they might think. Before I began, I ensured that I had my style guide ready - with colours, fonts and spacing checked for accessibility.

Style Guide

Whilst working at the BBC, I have learnt the importance of having a style / design system. The BBC have the 'Global Experience Language', a system of reusable interaction patterns. Even though I was working solo on this project, I knew that creating a mini design system of my own will assist with saving time, keeping things consistent and improve the overall look of the project and be very beneficial for the developer building the application! As a developer myself, I am biased to the last point in particular - having repeatable components makes developing an application much less painful and time consuming.
